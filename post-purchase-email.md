# Post-Purchase Email Template

Drop this into Lemon Squeezy → **Store settings** → **Emails** → **Order confirmation**.

You can customize the subject line and the email body. Lemon Squeezy supports merge tags like `{{customer_name}}` and `{{license_key}}` — use them.

---

## Subject Line Options (pick one)

These are listed in order from "warmest" to "most professional." All work.

1. `Your Smooth Paycheck is unlocked, {{customer_name}}.`
2. `One number, coming up.`
3. `Welcome in. Your Smooth Paycheck key is inside.`
4. `Your Smooth Paycheck license is here.`

My pick: **#1**. Feels personal, references the product, and the comma after the name is the kind of detail people notice on a busy inbox day.

---

## Email Body

```
Hey {{customer_name}},

Thanks for buying Smooth Paycheck. Genuinely — there are a hundred shiny tools you could've spent $29 on, and you picked this one. I appreciate it.

Your license key:

  {{license_key}}

To unlock the app:

  1. Go to https://smoothpaycheck.com/app.html
  2. Paste the key into the box
  3. That's it — the app remembers you, so you only do this once per device

(One key, up to 5 devices. Phone, laptop, tablet, work computer — go wild.)

────────

The 60-second version of how to use it:

It walks you through 3 quick steps — your last few months of income,
your rough monthly expenses, and your tax rate. Then it spits out
ONE big number: the largest paycheck you can safely pay yourself
every month, even slow ones.

That number is the whole product. Everything else (the allocation
breakdown, the buffer target, the stability score) is just the
math showing its work.

When you're done, hit "Download PDF" and stick it on your fridge.
Or your bathroom mirror. Or wherever you'll see it next time you're
about to spend the entire $8,000 client payment on that thing
you definitely don't need.

────────

A few quick things:

→ If your numbers are messy or surprising, that's normal. Most
  freelancers don't realize how volatile their income actually is
  until they see it on a chart.

→ The number this gives you is intentionally conservative. It's
  calibrated to your slow months, not your average ones. You can
  always pay yourself more during a great month — just don't make
  that the new floor.

→ The app saves everything locally in your browser. Nothing leaves
  your device. (If you clear your browser data, your inputs go
  with it. The license key is the only thing you need to keep.)

────────

If anything breaks, or if a number looks weird, or if you just want
to say hi — reply to this email. It comes straight to me.

And if it changes how you think about your money, even a little,
I'd love to hear about it.

— [Your name]
   the person who built this

P.S. 30-day refund, no questions, no hard feelings. Just hit reply
and I'll send your money back same day. But you won't need it.

P.P.S. If you build something cool with the paycheck number you
got — paid off a card, hit a savings goal, finally took a real
vacation — tell me. Stories like that are the only reason I built
this thing.
```

## Why each part is here

**The opening line.** Saying "thanks" is table stakes. Saying "*there are a hundred shiny tools you could've spent $29 on*" is not. It acknowledges they had a choice and treats them like a peer, not a wallet.

**The license key, prominent and unmissable.** This is the only piece of information that *must* land. Don't bury it under marketing copy. Make it impossible to miss on a phone screen.

**The 3-step "how to use it" block.** Every digital purchase has post-purchase regret risk. The buyer wonders "did I just waste $29?" within ~5 minutes of paying. A clear, low-effort path to first value kills that doubt.

**The "60-second version."** Shows respect for their time, and reinforces the *real* value prop: not a tool, but a single trustworthy number.

**The "few quick things" block.** Pre-emptively addresses the objections that would otherwise generate support emails: "is the number too low?", "did my data get sent somewhere?", "where do my inputs go?"

**Reply-to-this-email.** Most indie product creators don't do this and miss out massively. The single best feedback channel you'll ever have is buyers replying to a real person within 5 minutes of getting their key. Make it easy.

**The two P.S. lines.** Studies of email open behavior show people read P.S. lines second-most-often, after subject lines. Use both — one for the refund (kills hesitation), one for the story request (seeds testimonials and word of mouth).

## The one-line follow-up email (send 7 days later)

Schedule this as an automated email in Lemon Squeezy (Store settings → Emails → Custom emails) to fire 7 days after purchase. Subject: `quick check-in`.

```
Hey {{customer_name}},

Quick check — did you get to a paycheck number that felt right?

(If yes, would you be willing to send me a one-line testimonial?
Even something like "finally know what I can pay myself" works.
I'll put your first name on it, no last name, no photo unless
you want.)

(If no, what got in the way? I'll fix it if I can.)

— [Your name]
```

This single email is responsible for ~70% of the social proof you'll ever collect. Send it.

## What NOT to put in the post-purchase email

A few things tempting indie founders that will reduce trust:

- **Upsells.** Don't. Not in the first email. Not even soft ones. Let them get value from what they bought first.
- **Newsletter signup.** They already gave you their email when they bought. Adding them to a list without asking is borderline shady. *If* you want a list, ask in the day-7 follow-up, not the receipt.
- **Affiliate program.** Same issue — asking too early reads as transactional. Wait until they've actually used the product and gotten value, then offer it.
- **A long company story / "founder note".** They didn't buy a story, they bought a paycheck calculator. Get them to value first; story comes later.
